## Problem Statement

People with a content or product idea have no cheap, honest way to find out it's weak *before* they invest time building it. The two things they do today both lie to them:

1. **They ask ChatGPT**, which is trained to be agreeable and reinforces whatever they bring it (sycophancy).
2. **They just start building**, which postpones the moment the idea proves inadequate.

Persona is a paid "idea rejection machine": paste an idea, get reactions from a panel of AI personas deliberately spread across a characteristic space (Sobol sampling) and grounded in real public buyer language (Tavily). Output is bucketed **Would buy / Neutral / Hard no**, you can drill into any single persona's reasoning, and the report leads with **the top objections to beat** — honesty reframed as ammunition, not a death sentence.

## Demand Evidence

**Currently near-zero, and this is the most important fact in the project.**
- At the hackathon where it was built, ~2 people walked up and wanted to try the demo. (Interest, not demand — a hackathon floor is built for curiosity.)
- One person with a real idea wanted to use it, but the founder doesn't remember her name and can't follow up.
- No demand exists off the hackathon floor. No one has paid, returned, or asked when it ships.

Implication: the #1 job is not features. It's putting this in front of real people with real ideas and reading whether they'll pay for an emotionally unpleasant answer.

## Status Quo

The real competitor is **free ChatGPT**, plus "just start building." Both flatter the user. The founder's own framing, which two independent models converged on, is that **people lack a cheap, honest "no" early.** Nobody is short on encouragement; they're short on structured, cheap rejection before they sink the time.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**First ICP (chosen for reachability and evidence speed, NOT market size):** indie developers the founder is already in contact with — 6+ warm contacts plus ~2,000 X followers. The founder is one of them (scratch-your-own-itch with a built-in distribution channel).

**Known risk (per Codex cold read):** reachable ≠ in pain. Indie devs often want permission and momentum, not rejection, so the product may be partly anti-aligned with their emotional need. Founder's counter (kept premise #3 with reasoning): the "hard no" objections double as an improvement list, which feeds momentum and can turn early users into testimonials.

**Backup ICP (expansion if the wedge flinches):** people with money on the line and accountability — performance marketers / ad buyers (ROAS-graded, test constantly), e-commerce sellers (naming/listing/hero decisions before launch). They validate frequently (credits work) and will swallow a "no" because being wrong costs them more than the ego hit.

**Narrowest wedge:** a single paid run that delivers one jolt — "here are the harshest, realest reasons this probably flops, and the objections to beat" — that makes someone glad they didn't ship it blind.

## Constraints

- Solo founder, building in free time while traveling through China, then relocating to northern Italy for an AI x consulting role.
- Bootstrapped, not VC-backed. Every run has real COGS (LLM tokens + Tavily call). Unit economics must work per-run.
- Technically strong; ships complex apps solo with Claude Code. Secondary goal is to *learn every component* of a money-making app, so the build path must teach, not just validate.

## Premises (all confirmed by founder)

1. The product is **"the cheapest honest no," not "accurate audience simulation."** Compete on decision-cost; never make an unprovable human-parity accuracy claim.
2. **Sycophancy is both the enemy and the wedge.** Sobol spread (diversity), Tavily grounding (realism), and forced buy/neutral/no buckets exist to make the panel demonstrably harsher than free ChatGPT. The difference must be felt in 30 seconds or there is no product.
3. **First ICP is reachable indie devs**, chosen for evidence speed. *Kept by founder with reasoning* (objections-as-ammunition → improvement → testimonials). Measured against a kill-line (see Success Criteria); backup ICP named above.
4. **Real demand is currently unproven.** Next job is getting it in front of real users, not adding features. Building hard now is procrastination with extra steps.
5. **Credits only work if each single run feels worth real money.** Low-frequency users mean no reliance on volume; output quality, not pricing cleverness, makes credits viable.

### Business-model decisions (founder-led, first-principles)
- **No free tier, no free accounts.** A free tier requires near-zero marginal cost, network effects, or VC fuel — Persona has none of the three, so freeloaders would just burn tokens. (Strong unit-economics call.) *(Note: "credits" below is a conceptual/marketing word in Phase 1 — there is no stored balance or ledger until Phase 2; see Phase 1 Implementation Decisions.)*
- **A free *taste* is kept as the conversion hook** (distinct from a free *user*): a cold stranger sees 2-3 of the harshest grounded reactions, then pays a credit to unlock the full panel, drill-ins, and objections report. Rate-limited to cap token spend on tire-kickers. This directly serves premise #2.
- **Warm cohort gets founder-granted credits**, not a free tier — this is the validation group inside the paid model.
- Pricing: pay-per-use **credits**, not subscription (right for spiky, project-based usage).

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: The Smoke Test (minimal viable)
Codex's thin paid test. One page → Tavily snippets → one LLM call → ~12 grounded skeptical reactions bucketed → report leads with objections → free taste then $9. No accounts, no credits, no Sobol.
- Effort S (human ~3 days / CC a weekend). Risk Low.
- Pro: fastest demand + willingness-to-pay evidence. Con: teaches little new; throwaway; no credit system (a stated learning goal).

### Approach B: The Full Build (ideal architecture / max learning)
Auth, credit ledger + Stripe webhooks, Sobol sampling, Tavily grounding, small-LLM persona reviews, clustering, per-persona drill-in, report, deploy + observability.
- Effort XL (human ~6 weeks / CC ~1-2 weeks). Risk Med-High.
- Pro: learns every component. Con: violates premise #4 (builds before demand proven); risk of polishing a corpse.

## Recommended Approach

### Approach C: Validation-Shaped Learning Build (sequenced) — CHOSEN
Do A, then *earn* B.

**Phase 1 (this week):** Ship the thin paid smoke test to the X audience, but treat it as a deliberate thin slice of a real money-making app: landing → input form → Tavily grounding → **cheap taste panel (3 reactions)** shown free → paywall (one-off checkout) → **full panel (12 reactions)** + drill-ins + objections-to-beat report → deploy → basic analytics. This slice alone teaches the full money loop. No free tier; warm cohort seeded via 100%-discount codes.

**Phase 2 (only if the kill-line passes):** Build the deep version — stored credit ledger, Sobol-sampled persona generation, richer per-persona drill-in, background jobs — guided by real paying users.

**Why C:** the only option that serves both goals at once. Phase 1 is a complete money-making app at thin width (full-loop learning *now*) and reads demand. Deep learning is earned by evidence, so no corpse-polishing. The objections-as-ammunition framing is the report's spine from day one. Cost is discipline: ship ugly first, resist building the credit ledger before anyone pays.

**Honest tradeoff (acknowledged):** Phase 1 is more than the cheapest possible demand test. A pure fake-door (a Stripe link behind a mockup) would read willingness-to-pay a day faster. Phase 1 deliberately builds the real thin loop instead, because the founder's stated secondary goal is to learn the full money stack. That trade — a little validation speed for real full-loop learning — is intentional, not an accident.

### Phase 1 Implementation Decisions (resolved — buildable as written)

These close the ambiguities an engineer would otherwise hit in hour one:

- **Taste architecture (the key one):** the free taste is its *own* cheap generation of **3 reactions from a small grounding set**, NOT a preview skimmed off the full panel. This caps token spend on tire-kickers (you never generate the full panel for a non-payer) AND avoids leaking the best content. The full 12-reaction panel + drill-ins + objections report is generated only *after* payment. Show the taste as "a sample of how harsh this gets," not "your 3 worst reviews."
- **Anonymous rate-limit for the taste:** IP + signed cookie bucket, N tastes/day (start N=2). It is trivially bypassable (incognito, new IP) and that's fine — set an explicit small daily burn budget (e.g., cap total taste generations/day) rather than chasing perfect enforcement. **When the daily budget is exhausted, don't dead-end a launch-spike visitor:** hide the taste and show "tastes are maxed out today — buy a full run for $9 to see everything now." That converts the overflow instead of bouncing it.
- **"Credit" semantics in Phase 1:** there is **no stored balance and no ledger**. One full run = one one-off payment. "Credits" stays a conceptual/marketing word until Phase 2 builds the real ledger.
- **Payment mechanism (resolves the charge-after-generation requirement):** use a **Stripe manual-capture PaymentIntent**, not a plain hosted link. Flow: user clicks unlock → authorize the PaymentIntent (hold, not charge) → generate the full panel server-side → on success, capture the payment and reveal; on failure (Tavily empty / LLM timeout / malformed buckets), retry once, then **void the authorization** (no charge, nothing to refund) and show an apology + free retry. This gives true charge-on-success without the refund path, and it teaches real Stripe (serves the learning goal). Fallback if manual capture is too much for week one: pay-first hosted link + auto-refund-on-failure via the Stripe API — simpler integration, but you eat full COGS on the rare failed run and own a refund path.
- **Founder grants:** 100%-discount promo codes (Stripe coupons) or a small hardcoded email allowlist that skips checkout. No ledger needed.
- **Pinned panel sizes:** taste = 3 reactions, full = 12 reactions. These are the numbers the COGS gate below is computed against.
- **Input schema:** `idea` (text, required, ≤500 chars), `target_buyer` (text, required, ≤120 chars), `price` (text/number, optional), `where_buyer_hangs_out` (text, optional, ≤120 chars).
- **Grounding abstraction:** ground personas in *buyer-language snippets* (forum/review/blog text about the problem and buyer), not dossiers on named individuals. Avoids the privacy/legal edge and is cleaner under Tavily's ToS. Confirm Tavily ToS permits the use before launch.

## Pre-Code Gates (resolve BEFORE writing Phase 1)

- **COGS gate (hard gate, not an open question):** compute per-run cost at the pinned sizes — full panel = 12 reactions + Tavily call, taste = 3 reactions + Tavily call. Confirm a single ~$9 paid run clears margin comfortably *after* accounting for the free tastes given away per conversion. **Planning assumption to make the gate a real number:** assume ~3% taste→pay conversion, i.e. ~33 free tastes burned per paid run (≈1 taste per visitor). So fully-loaded cost per sale ≈ (1 full panel) + (~33 taste panels) + (~34 Tavily calls). If that doesn't clear ~$9 with margin, either raise the price, shrink the taste, or cap the daily taste budget harder. Do not ship the price until this number is real.
- **"Harsher than ChatGPT" acceptance test (gates public launch):** blind-rate Persona's output against a raw ChatGPT prompt on the same idea. Pass condition: an independent rater judges Persona "more useful / more critical" on ≥7 of 10 ideas. Until it passes, premise #2 is unproven and you don't launch publicly. Candidate levers: skepticism priming, forced grounding to a critical snippet, a mandatory minimum "hard no" quota per panel, and an adversarial second pass that rewrites soft reactions.

## Open Questions

- Exact prompt structure that *reliably* hits the harshness bar above (iterate against the acceptance test).
- Taste-leak tuning: confirmed resolved in design (taste is a separate cheap 3-reaction sample, not the harshest of the full panel), but watch real conversion to see if the sample is too satisfying or too weak.

## Success Criteria (the kill-line — Codex's bar, adopted)

Seed ~20 people (warm cohort via granted credits + cold X traffic via the taste→paywall):
- **Pass:** 5+ run it on *real* ideas they care about, AND 2+ pay for another run unprompted (no chasing).
- **Fail:** they try once, say "cool," give polite feedback, and don't come back or pay.

If it fails with indie devs, do not conclude the product failed — re-run the same test with the backup ICP (ad buyers / e-com) before abandoning.

## Distribution Plan

- **Deliverable:** a web app. Distribution channel already exists — founder's ~2k X following + 6 warm indie-dev contacts. No app-store or package-manager problem.
- **Deploy/CI:** Vercel (or similar) with push-to-deploy. Phase 1 needs only basic deploy + analytics; observability and webhook hardening come in Phase 2.

## Dependencies

- Accounts/keys: LLM API, Tavily, Stripe or Lemon Squeezy, Vercel. Supabase is **optional in Phase 1** (use only for run logging / analytics if at all; the credit ledger that needs it is a Phase 2 concern).
- The "harsher than ChatGPT" acceptance test (see Pre-Code Gates) passing before any public launch — de-risks premise #2.
- COGS-vs-price computed and clearing margin (see Pre-Code Gates) before the price ships.
